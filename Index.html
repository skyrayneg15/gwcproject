Endangered Animals in New Jersey

Home Page

Endangered Species are groups of animals in immediate danger of becoming extinct, or died out. Animals may become threatened or endangered because of a loss or change in habitat, over-exploitation, predation, competition, disease, disturbance or contamination. In New Jersey, there are many endangered animals that need your help! By learning about these animals and why they have become endangered, you are already taking steps to make a difference. If we work together, maybe we can save these animals!

Our Mission

The goal of this website is to teach you about endangered animals in New Jersey. We also want to let you know that it is possible for you to make a difference. It is possible for you to save these animals if you try! You also can prevent future problems, like having more species face endangerment. Share this information with your family and friends. Together, we can save the animals and change the world!

Piping Plover

(Lin, Robert)
The piping plover is a little bird that can be found near the seashores. It has a black neck band and a black bar on its forehead. The feathers are light sandy-brown and white, which allow the bird to hide from predators on sandy beaches. The legs are and the beak are orange, and the beak has a black tip. Male plovers have darker colored and wider neck bands. Piping plovers reproduce on Atlantic Coast beaches like Sandy Hook and Cape May. Piping plovers live on coastal beaches and boundary islands. Plovers eat aquatic worms, small shellfish, beetles and larvae.

Kemp Ridley Sea Turtle

(Patterson, Seth)
The kemp ridley sea turtle is the smallest species of sea turtles. The short, heavy shell looks a little heart-shaped, with high spinal bumps and jagged edges. The turtle has a medium sized triangular head and a curved beak with large surfaces. Kemp ridley turtles are dwell mostly inside the Gulf of Mexico and the U.S. Atlantic coast. They are normally found in shallow waters. They have been seen inside the Barnegat Bay in New Jersey. Young Ridley Turtles will eat small crabs, plants, and snails. Adults mostly, but they also eat mollusks and shellfish.

Bobcat

(Simpson, Wayne)
The bobcat is about two feet tall and can weigh up to 20 pounds.Their fur can either be yellowish brown or reddish brownThey only live in North America, specifically in alpine and mixed forests, swamp regions in and near Florida, and desert in the southwestern United States. They are missing in the really tended areas of the northern mid-states, and were once very plentiful in New Jersey. Bobcats eat rabbits, rats, squirrels, birds, turkeys, and even young deer.

Northeastern Beach Tiger Beetle

(Drummond, Mike)
Northeastern beach tiger beetles are classified due to their brownish-green head and thorax. It has white or light tan forewings, usually with black lines.The North eastern beach tiger beetle has been erased from Rhode Island, Connecticut, and New York. Only one tiny population has survived in New Jersey. They are inside Gateway National Recreation Area. Their diet generally consists of aquatic invertebrates, crabs, and fish. They also capture and eat small amphipods, flies, and spiders.

Blue Spotted Salamander

(Patterson, Seth)
These salamanders are between 8 and 14 cm in length. They are thought to eat various arthropods and soft bodied invertebrates such as slugs and earthworms. Their skin is bluish-black, with blue and white flecks on its back, and bluish-white spots on the sides of its body and tail. Blue-spotted salamanders are mostly found in moist and hardwood forests. They are endangered because they are not freeze tolerant, like some other frog species, so they must hibernate somewhere that doesn't freeze.

Bronze Copper Butterfly

(Bronze Copper)
The bronze copper visit flowers so they have been seen taking nectar at blackberry and red clovers. They live in habitats with low, wet areas such as marshes, wet meadows, and ponds. The male butterflies are iridescent copper-brown and the females are yellow-orange on their wingspan with black spots.

Shortnose Sturgeon

(NOAA)
The Shortnose Sturgeon is a yellowish brown fish with a short and bluntly rounded snout, wide mouth, and heterocercal tail. They use their barbels to locate their food: eat sludge worms, aquatic insect larvae, plants, snails, shrimp, and crayfish. They are usually around two feet long. They can be found in river mouths, tidal rivers, estuaries, and bays.

Sperm Whale

(Harris, Martin)
The Sperm whale is a dark brownish gray whale with one blowhole centered at the top of its head. They have distinctive, receding jaw with many large teeth, and a flat tail.They can grow to be up to 69 feet long, and their head often takes up a third of their entire body. They live in every ocean and they eat squid, fish, and sharks.

The snake also has black and white rectangular markings that look like corn kernals. They grow to be around 2.5 to 4 ft. in length. They live in forests and burrow near water, and usually eat mice.

Southern Gray Treefrog

(Devin's Frog)
The habitat for the Southern Gray Treefrog species rely on both fresh-water ponds and hardwood forests. They were listed on the endangered list in 1979, due to loss of its required habitat and its limited distribution. Many Gray Treefrogs feast on insects and their larvae and will sometimes even eat some of their species if they are small enough to catch.

Indiana Bat

(Indiana Bat)
The Indiana Bat hibernates during the winter seasons. Their diet consists of bugs and insects. This species uses echolocation to locate their prey. Indiana Bats are sometimes woken up out of hibernation because people invade their roosting site and purposely kill the bats.

Why Are These Animals Endangered?

Many animals in New Jersey, like the ones you have previously learned about, are endangered for many different reasons. The main reasons are the following: habitat loss/changes, over-exploitation, predation, competition, disease, and disturbance/contamination. It is important to learn about species’ problems causing endangerment and extinction so that we are able to stop the current problems and prevent future problems.

Habitat Loss/Changes

Habitat changes or habitat losses are a major threat to many species of animals. This is when the places where animals live, such as forests or plains, are destroyed or impacted by humans. Humans often do this so that they can build or farm on the land. To fix this problem, protected areas are established to save species and their natural habitats. However, habitat loss continues to be a major problem as humans continue to expand their needs.

(Sparkman, Kevin)

Over-exploitation

Over-exploitation is the overuse of wild animal and plant species by people. An example of over-exploitation would be overfishing or overhunting. Humans kill animals for food, other products, and for sport. It becomes over-exploitation when humans kill too many animals of a species, which often causes endangerment.

(Gonzalo)

Predation

Predation is the relationship in which the predator, like the lion, eats the prey, the zebra. Predation causes the population of both predators and prey to balance. Therefore, the two depend on each other. If there is less prey population for the predator, then the predator population decreases as well. The balance of predation in nature affects populations. Human involvement affects populations also, so if one population is affected, then its predator or prey population is affected as well.

(Yong, Ed)

Competition

Competition is the interaction between animals when they require the same resource, such as food. The two types of competition are interspecific and intraspecific. Interspecific is competition between animals within a species. Intraspecific is competition between animals of different species. Both types of competition affect populations of species, since animals fight for their resources.

(Wilkin, Douglas)

Disease

Disease and illnesses often threaten wildlife populations greatly. Animals spread different dangerous diseases among animals in their species as well as among animals in different species. This may result in excessive animal deaths leading to endangerment and extinction.

(Ohio Department of Health)

Contamination

Contamination is the presence of undesirable substances in the natural environment. Contaminants often cause pollution in many forms. This is caused by human action such as litter, pesticides and fertilizer, burning of fossil fuels, and other forms of impurities. The effects are drastic, including garbage patches, algal blooms, acid rain, and water pollution. This not only affects the animals, producing endangered and extinct species, but it also impacts humans, causing disease and environmental destruction.

(Snowden, Scott)

What Can You Do To Help Them?

There are many ways you can help protect these animals and bring them off of the endangered list.

Here's how you can help save the world!

(NOAA)
  • Educate your family and friends about endangerment and extinction in your area.
  • Try to not buy or use plastic products, as they tend to find their way into the ocean, harming our aquatic wildlife.
  • Do your part to share the beach with certain animals, like the Piping Plover, because they tend to breed on certain parts of the beach. If you take your pets to the beach, make sure to keep a close eye on them. Dogs can be a threat to certain endangered beach animals, like the piping plover and the Ridley Sea turtle.
  • When driving on the roads, drive carefully and be alert for any wild Animals that may be on the street, especially at night.
  • Do not purchase any clothes that are made from animal fur.
  • If you see trash on the ground, pick it up and throw it in the trash or recycling bins.
  • Volunteer at local organizations that help clean up the environment and/or help the animals.

The Bald Eagle's Success Story

(Sommerfeldt, Chris)

The bald eagle is an iconic symbol for the United States. In the 1960's and 1970's, however, the bald eagle population became a concern due to the counry's excessive use of a synthetic insecticide called DDT. The bald eagle population grew after DDT was banned and the species was protected under the Endangered Species Act. In 2007, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service removed the Bald Eagle from the federal endangered species list.
